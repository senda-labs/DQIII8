# Narrative Fundamentals

## Definition
Narrative is the art and craft of storytelling — organizing events, characters, and ideas into a meaningful sequence that engages readers and conveys theme. Understanding narrative structure and technique is essential for fiction, screenwriting, journalism, content creation, and persuasive communication.

## Core Concepts

- **Story Structure:** Frameworks for organizing narrative:
  - Three-Act Structure: Setup (25%) → Confrontation (50%) → Resolution (25%). Inciting incident triggers Act 1 end; climax drives Act 3.
  - Hero's Journey (Campbell): Ordinary world → Call to adventure → Trials → Transformation → Return. Universal mythic pattern.
  - Five-Act (Freytag's Pyramid): Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Denouement.
- **Plot:** Sequence of causally connected events. Aristotelian unity: events must follow from each other by necessity or probability, not coincidence.
- **Conflict:** The engine of story. Person vs. person, person vs. self, person vs. society, person vs. nature, person vs. fate/technology. Conflict creates tension and drives character action.
- **Point of View (POV):** First person (I — intimate, limited), second person (you — unusual, immersive), third person limited (close to one character's perspective), third person omniscient (narrator knows all).
- **Pacing:** Control of narrative speed. Scene (slow, real-time detail) vs. summary (fast, compressed time). Dialogue speeds scenes; description can slow or accelerate. Tension, chapter breaks, and cliffhangers control reader engagement.
- **Theme:** The underlying meaning or central idea. Not the topic (love, war) but the statement (love requires sacrifice). Theme emerges from plot, character, and imagery working together.
- **Voice:** The distinct personality and style of a narrator or author. Tone (attitude toward subject), diction (word choice), syntax, and perspective combine to create voice.

## Narrative Techniques
- In medias res: Begin in the middle of action
- Foreshadowing: Hints at future events
- Flashback/flash-forward: Non-linear time manipulation
- Unreliable narrator: Narrator's account is questionable
- Show don't tell: Demonstrate through action and detail rather than stating

## Practical Applications
- **Content marketing:** Brand storytelling, case studies, founder narratives.
- **Screenwriting:** Logline → outline → script → production draft.
- **UX writing:** User journey as narrative, product onboarding as story arc.
- **Presentations:** Problem → stakes → solution is a narrative structure.
- **Journalism:** Inverted pyramid vs. narrative feature writing.

# Historical Method Fundamentals

## Definition
Historical method is the set of techniques and principles historians use to research, evaluate, and present evidence about the past. It combines source criticism, contextual analysis, and interpretive frameworks to construct reliable accounts of historical events and processes.

## Core Concepts

- **Primary vs. Secondary Sources:** Primary sources are direct evidence from the period under study (documents, artifacts, eyewitness accounts, photographs, official records). Secondary sources are later interpretations and analyses of primary sources. Historians prioritize primary sources but synthesize secondary literature for context.
- **Source Criticism (Heuristics):** External criticism: Is the source authentic? When and where was it produced? Is it the original or a copy? Internal criticism: Is the content reliable? Was the author present? What were their biases, motives, and access to information?
- **Historical Interpretation:** Multiple valid interpretations of the same evidence are possible. Schools of interpretation: Marxist (class and economic forces), Annales (long-term structures, geography), social history (everyday people), postcolonial (power and empire), cultural history (meaning and representation).
- **Causation:** Historians distinguish proximate causes (immediate triggers) from underlying causes (structural factors). Avoid monocausal explanations. Counterfactual reasoning ("what if?") helps isolate causal factors.
- **Periodization:** Dividing history into periods is interpretive, not natural. Periods serve analytical purposes but can obscure continuities. Question what a periodization includes and excludes.
- **Historiography:** The history of historical writing — how interpretations of the same events have changed over time. Understanding historiography contextualizes current scholarship.
- **Archives and Research:** Finding aids, catalogues, digitization. Paleography (reading old scripts), diplomatics (authenticating documents), numismatics (coins), archaeology as supplementary disciplines.

## Key Questions Historians Ask
- What happened? When? Where? Who was involved?
- Why did it happen? What caused it?
- What were the consequences? For whom?
- How do we know? What evidence survives and why?
- Whose perspectives are represented? Whose are missing?

## Practical Applications
- **Journalism and fact-checking:** Evaluating sources, distinguishing primary evidence from hearsay.
- **Legal history:** Precedent research, understanding legislative intent.
- **Corporate history:** Institutional memory, due diligence on legacy liabilities.
- **Policy analysis:** Learning from historical precedents (pandemic responses, economic crises).
- **Digital humanities:** Computational text analysis, mapping, network analysis of historical data.

# Narrative Craft — Basics
**Domain:** Humanities & Arts / Literature
**Tier:** FREE — included in community edition

## Three-Act Structure

The most fundamental narrative framework in Western storytelling.

### Act I — Setup (roughly 25% of story)
Establish the world, the protagonist, and the status quo.
The inciting incident disrupts the equilibrium and poses the central question.
Ends with the protagonist committing to a course of action.

### Act II — Confrontation (roughly 50% of story)
The protagonist pursues their goal and faces escalating obstacles.
The midpoint shifts the direction — new information or a reversal of fortune.
The darkest moment (crisis) arrives near the end, testing the protagonist's resolve.

### Act III — Resolution (roughly 25% of story)
The climax — the final confrontation where the central conflict is resolved.
The protagonist is changed by the journey. Loose threads close.

## Character Arc

A character arc is the internal transformation a character undergoes.

**Positive arc** — Character overcomes a flaw or false belief. They grow.
**Negative arc** — Character fails to change, doubles down on the flaw, or corrupts.
**Flat arc** — Character does not change, but changes the world around them.

The arc is driven by:
1. A wound or lie the character believes (the internal problem)
2. Want vs Need — what they think they want vs what they actually need
3. Choices under pressure that reveal and define character

## Show, Don't Tell

One of the most cited principles in narrative craft.

**Tell:** "She was angry."
**Show:** "She set the cup down so carefully it didn't make a sound."

The principle: convey emotion, character, and meaning through specific sensory detail,
action, and dialogue — not through direct authorial declaration.

When to break it: exposition that would slow the story if dramatized, or when
the telling is itself the voice (unreliable narrators, distance, irony).

## Additional Principles

**Scene vs Summary** — Scenes dramatize important moments in real time.
Summaries compress time. Use scenes for the moments that matter most.

**Tension** — Every scene should have a question the reader wants answered.
Tension is not conflict alone — it is uncertainty about outcome.

**Voice** — The distinctive way a story is told. Word choice, rhythm, what is
noticed and what is omitted. Voice is what makes prose feel alive.

**Specificity** — "A dog" is weak. "A three-legged beagle with a chewed ear" is real.
Specific details create the illusion of truth.

# Macroeconomics Fundamentals

## Definition
Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole — aggregate output, employment, inflation, economic growth, and the monetary and fiscal policies that influence them. It operates at the national and global level.

## Core Concepts

- **GDP (Gross Domestic Product):** Total market value of all final goods and services produced in a country in a given period. Measured via expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X-M). Real GDP adjusts for inflation.
- **Inflation:** Sustained increase in the general price level. Measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index) and PCE. Causes: demand-pull (excess demand), cost-push (supply shock), monetary (M*V = P*Q). Central banks target ~2%.
- **Unemployment:** Types: frictional (between jobs), structural (skills mismatch), cyclical (recession-driven). Natural rate = frictional + structural. Full employment does not mean zero unemployment.
- **Business Cycles:** Alternating phases of expansion and contraction. Peak → recession (2 consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth) → trough → recovery. Leading indicators: building permits, stock prices, yield curve.
- **Monetary Policy:** Central bank control of money supply and interest rates. Tools: open market operations (buy/sell bonds), policy rate (federal funds rate, repo rate), reserve requirements, quantitative easing. Transmission: rates → borrowing → investment → AD.
- **Fiscal Policy:** Government spending and taxation. Expansionary: increase G or cut taxes to stimulate economy. Contractionary: reverse. Multiplier effect: change in GDP > initial change in spending.
- **Aggregate Demand / Aggregate Supply (AD-AS):** AD slopes down (wealth, interest rate, exchange rate effects). SRAS slopes up. LRAS is vertical at potential output. Stagflation: AS shock shifts SRAS left — higher prices, lower output.
- **International Trade:** Comparative advantage: countries gain from specializing and trading even if one is absolutely better at everything. Balance of payments = current account + capital account.

## Key Indicators
- GDP growth rate, inflation rate, unemployment rate, current account balance
- Yield curve (shape predicts recessions), M2 money supply, credit growth

## Practical Applications
- **Investment:** Interest rate cycles drive bond prices and equity valuations.
- **Currency trading:** Macro differentials (rates, growth, inflation) drive FX.
- **Business planning:** Forecasting demand using macro conditions.
- **Government policy:** Stimulus packages, austerity measures, central bank decisions.

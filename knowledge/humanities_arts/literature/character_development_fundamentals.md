# Character Development Fundamentals

## Definition
Character development is the process of creating, deepening, and transforming fictional characters so that they feel real, complex, and memorable. Strong characters drive plot, embody theme, and form the emotional connection between story and reader.

## Core Concepts

- **Character Dimensions:** E.M. Forster's distinction: flat characters (one-dimensional, consistent, useful as types) vs. round characters (complex, contradictory, capable of surprising). Protagonists and major characters should be round.
- **Character Arc:** The internal journey of transformation. Positive arc: character overcomes flaw to become better. Negative arc: character fails to change (tragedy) or chooses wrong change. Flat arc: character remains constant but changes the world around them.
- **Want vs. Need:** External desire the character consciously pursues (want/goal) vs. what the character unconsciously lacks and must learn (need/wound/flaw). The story forces the character to choose between them at the climax.
- **Backstory and Wound:** The character's history — particularly a formative wound or lie they believe about themselves or the world — shapes their current behavior, fears, and desires. Backstory should inform but not dominate present action.
- **Agency:** Characters must make consequential choices that reveal character. Choices under pressure are the most revealing. Avoid characters who are passive recipients of plot events.
- **Voice and Speech Patterns:** Each character should speak distinctly. Vocabulary, sentence structure, topics of interest, evasions, and verbal tics differentiate characters in dialogue.
- **Relationships and Foils:** Characters reveal themselves through contrast. A foil highlights opposite traits in the protagonist. Relationships create conflict and force characters to change.
- **Motivation:** Characters need clear, believable reasons for every significant action. Even villains believe they are justified. "The character who wants something — even a glass of water" (Vonnegut).

## Techniques
- Character questionnaires: Deep background (even if never used in text)
- Write scenes from other characters' POV to understand their perspective
- Physical embodiment: Mannerisms, posture, speech patterns, appearance
- Internal monologue: Show the gap between what characters think and say/do

## Practical Applications
- **Fiction writing:** Protagonists, antagonists, supporting cast for novels and short stories.
- **Screenwriting:** Character bibles for TV series, development through episodes.
- **Games:** NPC design, player character customization and arc in RPGs.
- **Marketing personas:** User personas borrow character development techniques.
- **Branding:** Brand voice and personality as character design.

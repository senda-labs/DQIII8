# Ethics Fundamentals

## Definition
Ethics (moral philosophy) is the systematic study of what is morally right and wrong, good and bad, just and unjust. It provides frameworks for evaluating actions, policies, and character, and for resolving moral conflicts through reasoned argument.

## Core Concepts

- **Consequentialism:** Rightness of an action is determined by its outcomes. Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill): maximize total happiness/welfare. Act utilitarianism: evaluate each act. Rule utilitarianism: follow rules that maximize utility. Challenge: measuring and comparing welfare; justifies troubling sacrifices for aggregate benefit.
- **Deontology:** Rightness is determined by adherence to rules or duties, regardless of consequences. Kant's categorical imperative: "Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become universal law." Rights-based: some actions are forbidden even if they produce good outcomes.
- **Virtue Ethics:** Focuses on character rather than rules or outcomes. Aristotle: virtues (courage, honesty, justice, temperance) are excellences of character developed through practice. The virtuous person perceives situations correctly and acts appropriately. Eudaimonia (flourishing) as the goal.
- **Contractarianism:** Moral principles are those rational agents would agree to from behind a "veil of ignorance" (Rawls) — not knowing their social position. Justice as fairness: difference principle (inequalities justified only if they benefit the least advantaged).
- **Care Ethics:** (Gilligan, Noddings) Emphasizes relationships, care, and context over abstract universal principles. Morality grounded in particular relationships rather than impartial rules.
- **Applied Ethics:** Applying ethical frameworks to real-world issues: bioethics (end-of-life decisions, genetic engineering), AI ethics (bias, autonomy, privacy), business ethics, environmental ethics.
- **Metaethics:** Questions about the nature of moral claims. Moral realism (objective moral facts exist), anti-realism (moral claims are not objective), relativism (morality is culturally determined).

## Key Distinctions
- Descriptive ethics vs. normative ethics vs. metaethics
- Moral vs. legal (legal ≠ moral)
- Supererogatory acts: beyond what duty requires (heroism, exceptional generosity)

## Practical Applications
- **AI development:** Fairness, accountability, transparency, privacy in ML systems.
- **Business decisions:** Stakeholder ethics, corporate social responsibility.
- **Policy:** Justifying redistribution, healthcare allocation, environmental regulation.
- **Personal life:** Everyday moral reasoning, integrity, professional ethics.
- **Research:** IRB (Institutional Review Board) ethical review of studies.

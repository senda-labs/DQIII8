# Sugars and Glycemic Index

## Added Sugar Limits
```
AHA recommendations:
  Women: ≤25g/day (6 tsp)
  Men:   ≤36g/day (9 tsp)
WHO: <10% total calories; <5% for additional benefit
```
- Total sugars vs. added sugars: EU labels show total only; US labels (post-2020) show both
- Naturally occurring sugars (fruit, milk) are not added sugars

## Glycemic Index (GI)
```
Low:    GI < 55   (lentils ~32, oats ~55)
Medium: GI 56–69  (brown rice ~68, banana ~62)
High:   GI > 70   (white bread ~75, glucose = 100)
```
GI measures blood glucose response relative to pure glucose.
Factors that lower GI: fat, protein, fiber, acid (vinegar), cooking method (al dente vs. overcooked).

## Glycemic Load (GL)
```
GL = (GI × carbohydrates_g) / 100
```
| Category | GL per serving |
|----------|---------------|
| Low | <10 |
| Medium | 11–19 |
| High | ≥20 |

GL is more practical than GI — accounts for realistic portion size.
Example: watermelon GI=76 (high), but GL=4 per 120g serving (low).

## Fructose Metabolism
```
Safe daily limit: ~50g fructose
HFCS and sucrose: 50% fructose by weight
```
- Fructose is metabolized exclusively by the liver (unlike glucose)
- Excess fructose → de novo lipogenesis → hepatic steatosis (fatty liver)
- Liquid fructose (soda, juice) is more harmful than whole-fruit fructose (fiber slows absorption)

## Sugar Alcohols (Polyols)
| Compound | kcal/g | GI | Laxative threshold |
|----------|--------|----|--------------------|
| Erythritol | 0.2 | 0 | >50g (best tolerated) |
| Xylitol | 2.4 | 7 | >20g |
| Sorbitol | 2.6 | 9 | >20g |
| Maltitol | 2.1 | 35 | >30g |

## Non-Nutritive Sweeteners (FDA GRAS status)
| Sweetener | Source | Sweetness vs sucrose | Notes |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|-------|
| Sucralose | Synthetic | 600× | Heat stable, baking-compatible |
| Stevia (rebaudioside) | Plant | 200–400× | Natural, slight aftertaste |
| Aspartame | Synthetic | 200× | Breaks down at high temp; avoid in PKU |
| Acesulfame-K | Synthetic | 200× | Often blended with aspartame |

No direct caloric effect. Some evidence of gut microbiome disruption at high doses (under review).
